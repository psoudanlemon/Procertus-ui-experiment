## The physical page

Unlike screen interfaces that flex and scroll, a printed document is a fixed
canvas. The A4 sheet at 210 x 297mm is the only format the platform produces,
and its physical constraints shape every layout decision. Content that looks
fine on screen can clip, bleed, or become illegible once it hits paper.

<div className="grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-6">
  <div className="rounded-xl border border-border bg-card p-6">
    <h4 className="font-sans text-base font-bold text-foreground m-0 mb-3">Print margins</h4>
    <p className="text-sm text-muted-foreground leading-[1.6] m-0">
      Physical printers clip unpredictably near sheet edges, and regulatory
      bodies expect consistent, professional margins on filed documents. The
      PROCERTUS system enforces a 20mm safe zone on all four edges at the
      renderer level. No content, no decorative elements, no bleed. Template
      authors cannot override this. It reduces the usable content area to
      170 x 257mm, and every grid, header, and footer must fit within that
      boundary.
    </p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border border-border bg-card p-6">
    <h4 className="font-sans text-base font-bold text-foreground m-0 mb-3">Point-based typography</h4>
    <p className="text-sm text-muted-foreground leading-[1.6] m-0">
      Documents use typographic points, not CSS pixels, because the output is
      paper, not a viewport. Body text floors at 11pt for readability. Dense
      data rows in T2 documents can go as low as 7pt, but no smaller. Footers
      sit at 6pt, light enough to recede but always legible when a regulator
      needs to trace a page back to its generation timestamp.
    </p>
  </div>
</div>

## The authority ladder

Every document the platform generates belongs to exactly one tier. The tier is
not a style preference. It encodes the document's *authority*, its intended
audience, and how long it will exist in the world. A certificate pinned to an
office wall demands different treatment than an internal export headed for a
shredder.

## The template contract

Within each tier, multiple document types exist. An audit report, a corrective
action request, and a surveillance summary are all Tier 2 documents, but they
serve different purposes and carry different content. The tier defines the
*visual rules*. The template defines the *content structure*.

Templates are interchangeable within their tier. Any Tier 2 template can be
swapped for another Tier 2 template without breaking the visual language,
because they all inherit the same logotype placement, color mode, grid system,
and typographic hierarchy. This is the fundamental design constraint: the tier
is the contract, and templates are implementations of that contract.

## Tier anatomy

Each tier prescribes a specific arrangement of structural elements. These are
not suggestions for template designers. They are fixed positions that every
document within the tier must honor.
